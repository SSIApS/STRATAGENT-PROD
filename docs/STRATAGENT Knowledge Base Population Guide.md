# STRATAGENT Knowledge Base Population Guide
### How to Feed the Research Engine for Maximum Intelligence Output

---

## The Core Principle

STRATAGENT's research agents match your supplier's capabilities against a prospect's world. The richer the match surface, the higher the Convergence Index. A KB that says "we sell industrial pumps" gives the agent almost nothing to work with. A KB that describes *which pumps perform in which conditions, certified to which standards, used by which types of companies, solving which specific operational problems* — that gives the agent dozens of entry points to find the right prospect at the right moment.

Think of it this way: **you are briefing a senior sales researcher who has never met your supplier and is about to spend a week on the internet finding you the best opportunities they can.** Everything you give them makes them smarter.

## The Eight Intelligence Elements — What Each One Really Needs

### 1. Product Catalogue
**What most people put:** "We sell conveyor belt components, marking systems, and industrial labels."  
**What makes it powerful:**

- Full product names with model numbers or series names
- The *operating envelope* — temperature range, pressure rating, speed, load capacity, chemical resistance
- Material specifications — what the product is made of and why that matters (e.g. "PEEK polymer housing rated for continuous use at 260°C, resisting chlorinated solvents")
- Size/format range — smallest to largest, custom vs standard
- What it connects to or integrates with — adjacent systems, interfaces, compatibility
- What it *replaces* — older technologies, competitor products, manual processes it eliminates

**Why it matters:** The agent searches for companies operating in conditions that match your product's envelope. If you make heat-resistant marking systems rated to 400°C, the agent can find steel mills, foundries, and ceramic kilns. Without the temperature rating, it finds nothing specific.

---

### 2. Technical Differentiators
**What most people put:** "High quality, long lasting, good customer service."  
**What makes it powerful:**

- Performance edge in a *specific measurable situation* — "outperforms competitor X by 40% in humidity above 85%"
- Failure mode it eliminates — "prevents barcode smearing on parts entering acid baths"
- The scenario where competitors lose and you win — be brutally specific
- Installation or integration advantage — faster to fit, no special tools, works with existing PLC
- Lifetime or maintenance advantage — hours between service, replacement cost comparison
- The one thing a procurement manager remembers after the meeting

**Why it matters:** Buying triggers are often failures. If a plant manager just lost a batch because their current labels dissolved in a wash cycle, they are searching for exactly what you do. The agent can only connect those dots if it knows what failure your product prevents.

---

### 3. Certifications & Compliance
**What most people put:** "ISO 9001 certified."  
**What makes it powerful:**

- Full certification names with scope — "ATEX II 2G Ex ia IIC T4 Gb for use in Zone 1 explosive atmospheres"
- Certification body and certificate number if available
- Geographic recognition — CE (Europe), UL (North America), DNV (marine/offshore), GOST (Russia/CIS)
- Industry-specific standards — FDA 21 CFR for food contact, DNV GL for offshore, EN 13501 for fire classification, REACH/RoHS for chemical compliance
- What the certification *unlocks* — "required by law in any EU facility handling flammable gases above 10 litres"
- Pending certifications and expected date

**Why it matters:** Regulatory deadlines are the highest-quality buying triggers that exist. A company that must achieve ATEX compliance by a deadline is *forced* to buy. The agent can find these companies and timed pressures only if it knows which regulations your certifications satisfy.

---

### 4. Case Studies & Reference Projects
**What most people put:** Nothing, or "available on request."  
**What makes it powerful:**

- Industry sector and country (company name optional if confidential)
- The exact operational challenge they had before your product
- What they tried before that didn't work
- The specific installation context — temperature, environment, throughput volume, line speed
- Measurable outcome — downtime reduced by X%, labels lasting 3x longer, zero rejects in 12 months
- Time to ROI if known
- Quote from the customer if available

**Why it matters:** Case studies are the agent's proof-of-concept library. When it finds a prospect in the same sector with the same operational environment, it can say "we have a reference installation in exactly this context." That converts a 65 CI to an 85.

---

### 5. Buyer Profiles
**What most people put:** "We sell to manufacturing companies."  
**What makes it powerful:**

- Job titles of the actual decision makers and influencers — "Plant Manager initiates, Maintenance Engineer specifies, Procurement Manager approves"
- What keeps each persona up at night — their KPIs, their risks, their career pressures
- The language they use (not your language) — what words do they search for, what do they call the problem
- Where they find suppliers — trade shows, directories, LinkedIn, specific magazines or industry bodies
- Budget cycle timing — Q4 capex freeze vs Q1 fresh budget
- How long the sales cycle typically runs and what accelerates it
- Who else influences the decision — HSE Manager, Quality Director, Operations Director

**Why it matters:** The agent needs to find the right person's name and title at the prospect company. If it knows the buyer is always a "Maintenance & Reliability Manager" or "Head of HSEQ," it searches for that specific title. Precision targeting changes a cold approach into a warm one.

---

### 6. Competitive Positioning
**What most people put:** Nothing.  
**What makes it powerful:**

- Names of the top 3 competitors the supplier encounters most often
- Where each competitor wins (be honest — this helps the agent avoid bad fits)
- Where the supplier wins and specifically *why* in that situation
- Price positioning — premium, mid-market, or value, and what justifies it
- Situations where the supplier should *not* be recommended — the honest gate
- What prospects say when they choose a competitor instead

**Why it matters:** Knowing the competitive landscape lets the agent qualify prospects correctly. If a prospect is already a flagship customer of the market leader and has a 5-year contract, the agent can park them for later rather than burning an action on a dead end.

---

### 7. Operational Context (Industries & Applications)
**What most people put:** "We serve manufacturing, oil & gas, and food & beverage."  
**What makes it powerful:**

- Specific sub-sectors within each industry — not "oil & gas" but "subsea manifold assembly, FPSO topsides, LNG terminal logistics"
- Specific processes within those operations where the product is used — "applied at the end of the galvanising line before quench tank"
- Geographic strongholds — where the supplier is already established and can reference-sell
- Geographic expansion targets — where they want to grow but have no presence yet
- Facility types — greenfield plants vs. retrofit projects vs. maintenance contracts
- Typical project sizes and contract structures

**Why it matters:** The agent searches for companies running specific processes in specific geographies. The more precise your operational context, the tighter the prospect list — and a list of 10 excellent prospects beats a list of 100 generic ones.

---

### 8. Objections & Responses
**What most people put:** Nothing.  
**What makes it powerful:**

- The 5 most common reasons a prospect says no or delays
- The honest answer to each (not marketing spin)
- Proof points that neutralise each objection — data, certifications, reference customers
- The question that reopens a stalled deal
- What the prospect is really worried about beneath the stated objection

**Why it matters:** The agent uses this to assess whether a prospect's known concerns are addressable before the approach is made. It also feeds directly into proposal and value brief generation — the output documents anticipate and pre-empt resistance.

## Additional Power Inputs

These don't map to a specific intelligence element but dramatically improve research quality:

### Recon Drops (Field Intelligence as you travel)
When you visit a trade show, a client, or a prospect's facility, drop a voice note or photo into the supplier's folder. Examples:
- "Spoke with the plant manager at Aalborg — they're replacing their entire marking system next year, budget approved, waiting on a vendor decision"
- Photo of a competitor's product installed at a site you visited
- A trade magazine article about a regulation coming into force in your target market

These become intelligence the agent can cross-reference against existing watched prospects.

### LinkedIn Company Pages
Add the supplier's LinkedIn URL as a web source. The agent extracts company description, employee count, recent posts, and follower demographics — all useful for positioning and understanding how the company presents itself vs. how it actually operates.

### Distributor and Partner Network
If the supplier sells through distributors in certain markets, list them. The agent avoids targeting a prospect already being served by a known distributor and instead finds the gaps.

### Pricing Framework (Even a Range)
Even a rough indication — "typically €500–€5,000 per order, larger installations €50k+" — helps the agent filter prospects by likely deal size. A sole trader cannot be a viable prospect for a €200k minimum project.

### Upcoming Trade Shows or Events
If the supplier will exhibit at a trade show in three months, the agent can identify prospects attending the same event and prioritise them for outreach before the show.

## The Compound Effect

Each data point you add doesn't just improve one search — it improves every future search. A certification added today might not create a match tomorrow, but when a new regulation passes in six months and a prospect comes under compliance pressure, the agent already knows your supplier is the solution.

The goal is to get every supplier KB above **80 Intelligence Depth** (PROPOSAL READY). At that level, the agent has enough surface area to find high-confidence opportunities, and the output documents can be sent with minimal editing.

**The fastest path to 80+:**
1. Add the supplier website as a web source (instant +10–15 points)
2. Upload one product datasheet PDF (+8–12 points)
3. Upload one case study PDF (+10–15 points)
4. Add LinkedIn company page URL (+5–8 points)
5. Fill in the certifications field manually if the website doesn't list them clearly

That sequence alone typically moves a supplier from INTELLIGENCE GAP to VALUE BRIEF READY in under 20 minutes.

## Folder Structure for Offline Sync

Drop files into the supplier's local folder and run **Sync Now** to ingest them automatically:

```
Suppliers/
  [Supplier Name]/
    Product/          → product datasheets, spec sheets, brochures
    Case Studies/     → application notes, project summaries, testimonials
    Certifications/   → certificate PDFs, test reports, compliance docs
    Campaigns/        → competitor comparisons, positioning documents
    Markets/          → industry guides, sector reports, regulatory updates
    Prospects/        → (skipped by sync — Field Intelligence data only)
```

Name the supplier folder *exactly* as the KB company name for sync to work.
